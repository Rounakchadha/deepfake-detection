## 1. Executive Summary

In our experiments, we evaluated two primary architectures: a lightweight **MesoNet** model and a deeper **XceptionNet** model using transfer learning. Both models were trained and evaluated on a combination of the **FaceForensics++**, **Celeb-DF**, and **DFDC** datasets.

Overall, the **XceptionNet** model demonstrated superior performance in terms of accuracy and generalization, likely due to its deeper architecture and the power of transfer learning. However, **MesoNet** proved to be a highly competitive and efficient alternative, making it suitable for applications with limited computational resources.

## 5. Error Analysis

### False Positives (Real faces classified as FAKE)
We observed that false positives sometimes occurred in images with:
- Unusual lighting conditions.
- Non-frontal face poses.
- Blurring or low resolution.

### False Negatives (Fake faces classified as REAL)
False negatives were more common for:
- High-quality deepfakes from the Celeb-DF dataset.
- Videos where the manipulation was very subtle or limited to a small region of the face.
- Deepfakes generated by newer, more advanced techniques not present in the training data.

---

## 6. Conclusion

The results demonstrate that both MesoNet and XceptionNet are effective at detecting deepfakes, with XceptionNet consistently outperforming MesoNet across most metrics. The success of the transfer learning approach with XceptionNet highlights the value of leveraging features learned from large-scale image datasets.

For a production system where accuracy is paramount, **XceptionNet is the recommended model**. For applications where computational efficiency and speed are more critical, **MesoNet offers a compelling and robust alternative**.

Future work could explore hybrid models (e.g., CNN-LSTM) to better capture temporal artifacts in videos, as well as incorporating more recent and advanced deepfake datasets for training.
